Key terms explained

APR (Annual Percentage Rate)
The true annual cost of borrowing, incorporating the base interest rate and any mandatory upfront fees. The single most useful number for comparing loans.
Fixed vs Variable rate
Fixed rates stay constant for the full term — predictable but sometimes higher. Variable rates move with an index (RPI, CPI, bank rate) — can fall, but can also rise unexpectedly.
Income-contingent repayment
Repayments are calculated as a percentage of your income above a threshold. If you earn less, you pay less. Remaining debt is written off after the term ends.
Early repayment charge (ERC)
A penalty some lenders charge if you repay the loan ahead of schedule. None of the systems here carry an ERC — you can always overpay without penalty.
Loan term
How long you have to repay. Longer terms lower monthly payments but increase total interest paid. Income-contingent systems have soft write-off deadlines instead.
Total amount payable
Every payment you will ever make — principal + interest + fees. This is the real cost of the debt, and the number to compare when choosing between two loan offers.

Price tags only tell part of the story. Until you hit the wall of trying to put a price tag on what kind of additional value you get by entering a decent university, the numbers above look deceptively simple.

🏛️

Prestige & alumni networks don't always cost more

Elite US universities — Ivy League, Stanford, MIT, the UC system — and prestigious institutions across Asia and Europe are famous for their alumni networks and career reputations. Yet their tuition often mirrors lower-tier schools in the same country. The price difference can be surprisingly small compared to the perceived gap in outcomes.

Exchange opportunities money can't always buy

Some universities offer cross-border academic mobility that no tuition fee can simply be "paid for." Students at universities within the European Higher Education Area (EHEA) can transfer credits, attend partner institutions, and access the Erasmus+ network — sometimes without even using their passport for intra-Schengen moves.

This frictionless mobility is a structural benefit that goes well beyond cost. A student at a French public university paying ~€2,850/year may have access to academic networks spanning 50+ countries — something a higher-fee institution elsewhere may struggle to replicate.

EU Erasmus+ covers 30+ countries · 800,000+ students annually
What this dashboard doesn't capture: Graduate earnings premiums by institution, social capital and network effects, research output quality, on-campus experience, mental health support, internship pipelines, and post-study work visa rights (which vary significantly — e.g. UK Graduate Route, Australia's post-study work stream, OPT in the US). These intangibles often matter as much as — or more than — the raw tuition figure. The data above is a starting point, not a complete picture.
